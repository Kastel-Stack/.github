
# Kastel Stack: An Invitation to Build Something Different
White Paper · May 2026

You don't need to have read any prior Kastel material. Everything you need to understand the project is explained here.

## The Problem with How AI Is Being Built

Most AI tools being deployed in businesses today are built around a single idea: **do more, faster, at lower cost**. Automate the workflow. Optimise the metric. Scale the output. Trust the platform.

This is not a bad instinct. Efficiency matters. But when we examine what this approach actually produces over time, a pattern emerges that should concern us.

Businesses become dependent on platforms they don't control. Decisions get handed to systems that can't be audited. Operators — the founders, specialists and independent practitioners who built something real — find themselves managing tools they don't fully understand, producing outputs they can't always verify, in service of growth metrics that may have nothing to do with what made their business worth building in the first place.

The dominant model of enterprise AI can be summarised as: **make the organisation computable, then automate it**.

Kastel Stack starts from a different question entirely.

---

## A Different Question

Instead of asking *"how do we automate this business?"*, Kastel asks:

> **What would it mean for a business to become genuinely more intelligent?**

This sounds simple. It is not. Most uses of the word "intelligent" in technology today mean something closer to "faster" or "more automated". Kastel draws on a richer body of research — called **Trident-G theory** — to define intelligence in a more demanding way.

Here is the core idea, stated plainly:

**Intelligence is not about doing more. It is about staying capable across a wider range of conditions — while remaining truthful, grounded, and human-governed.**

Think of a skilled consultant, an experienced founder, or a great specialist practitioner. What makes them genuinely intelligent is not that they execute tasks quickly. It is that they can:

- Read a situation accurately, even when the signals are noisy or ambiguous
- Generate good hypotheses about what is really happening
- Test those hypotheses with small, careful probes before committing to action
- Learn from feedback in a way that transfers to new situations
- Know when they don't know enough to act
- Refuse to be captured by easy answers or powerful clients

Kastel Stack is an attempt to build that kind of intelligence into the operating infrastructure of independent businesses — without replacing the human judgement at the centre.

## What Trident-G Theory Actually Says

*This section explains the theoretical foundation. The terminology is explained as it appears.*

Trident-G is a cognitive and adaptive systems theory developed by the team behind the IQ Mindware research programme. It provides the intellectual backbone for Kastel Stack. You don't need to master it to contribute, but understanding its core claims will help you understand why Kastel is built the way it is.

**The viability band (Ψ-band)**

Trident-G begins by arguing that the goal of an intelligent system is not to maximise any single output. It is to remain *viable* — that is, to stay within a zone of functioning from which it can still act, learn and recover. This zone is called the **Ψ-band** (Ψ is the Greek letter psi, used here as shorthand for psychological or systemic viability).

Think of it like body temperature. You don't want the highest possible body temperature. You want to stay within a healthy range. A business has an equivalent: a zone in which it can handle uncertainty, serve its customers, pay its bills, learn from mistakes and remain recognisably itself. Kastel's goal is to help businesses stay in that zone and *widen it over time* — not push them towards one maximised metric until they break.

**The niche**

Trident-G insists that intelligence cannot be understood in isolation. An intelligent agent always exists in relation to its *niche* — the specific environment, audience, market, community or context it inhabits. A decision that looks logical in the abstract may be wrong for a particular niche. Good intelligence is intelligence that stays *coupled* to its real niche: the actual customers, partners, competitors and conditions the business faces.

This matters enormously for AI. Most AI systems are designed to be general. They can be applied anywhere. But the questions that matter for a founder-led training company are not the same as those that matter for a craft manufacturer or a cross-border research consultancy. Kastel is designed to remain anchored to the specific niche of each business it serves, not to impose a generic growth playbook.

**Fluid intelligence and crystallised intelligence (Gf → Gc)**

Cognitive science distinguishes between two kinds of intelligence:

- **Fluid intelligence (Gf)** — the capacity to reason through *new* problems, under uncertainty, without established routines to fall back on. This is the intelligence you use when you encounter a situation you haven't seen before.
- **Crystallised intelligence (Gc)** — accumulated, tested knowledge: the routines, schemas, scripts and patterns that you've built up through experience and that you can apply reliably.

Both matter. Fluid intelligence without crystallised intelligence means you're always solving problems from scratch, with no accumulated wisdom. Crystallised intelligence without fluid intelligence means you apply old answers to new problems, unable to update when the world changes.

Trident-G argues that genuine intelligence involves a *spiral advance*: using fluid intelligence to navigate new situations, then carefully extracting from that experience the patterns that genuinely transfer — banking them as crystallised intelligence — so that future fluid reasoning starts from a richer base.

For a business, this looks like:

- Facing an uncertain situation (Gf: working it out in real time)
- Finding something that works
- Testing carefully whether it really works, or was just lucky
- If it genuinely works across contexts, documenting it as a reliable operating pattern (Gc: banked wisdom)
- Using those documented patterns as the foundation for future decision-making

This is mundane as a description. It is very hard to do well. Most businesses either don't extract lessons at all, or bank lessons too eagerly — treating one success as a permanent truth before it's been properly tested.

**Entropy and mutual information**

Trident-G frames intelligence as a balance between two forces:

- **Entropy** (in this context): the tendency towards exploration, experimentation, variation, search. Without enough entropy, a system gets stuck in a rut, doing the same thing while the world changes around it.
- **Mutual information**: the constraint that comes from having a clear, tested model of what works. Without enough constraint, a system scatters its effort across possibilities, never learning anything reliable.

Healthy intelligence holds these in balance. Too much exploration and you're noisy, unfocused, inconsistent. Too much exploitation and you're brittle, locked-in, unable to adapt. Kastel's design tries to hold this balance at the level of a business: enough experimentation to discover, enough discipline to bank what's real.

**Anti-capture sovereignty**

Trident-G includes a political-economic dimension that is unusual for a cognitive theory. It argues that genuine adaptive intelligence includes the capacity to *avoid capture* — to refuse dependence on any single platform, funder, client or institutional logic that would compromise the agent's ability to act freely.

For a business, this means: use platforms, but don't become hostage to them. Keep your data portable. Keep your judgement human. Grow replacement paths before you need them. Don't let the convenience of automation erode your ability to understand and govern your own operations.

This is not anti-technology. It is pro-sovereignty.

## What Kastel Stack Actually Builds

Kastel Stack is an open-source framework that applies Trident-G theory to the practical operations of independent businesses. Here is what it does, in concrete terms.

**It treats every business as a hypothesis being tested.**

A business is not a machine to be optimised. It is a model of how value can be created, delivered and sustained in a specific niche. That model should be made explicit, tested carefully, updated when evidence demands it, and refined over time. Kastel's first question to any business is not "how do we grow?" but "what model are we testing, and what would prove or disprove it?"

**It builds a structured picture of the business's current state.**

Kastel connects to the tools a business already uses — its code repository, its customer database, its payment processor, its email platform — and uses typed, defined data from these sources to build a clear picture of what is actually happening. Not a dashboard of vanity metrics. A grounded, semantic map of real business state.

*Typed data* means data that is clearly defined: not just a number, but a number that means something specific, sourced from one authoritative system, with a clear definition attached to it. This is harder to build than it sounds, and most businesses don't have it. Kastel is designed to create it.

**It diagnoses whether the business is ready to act.**

Before proposing any action, the G Kernel (Kastel's central reasoning engine) asks two questions:

1. Is the business internally ready to act? (Do we have the capacity, resources, clarity and governance to execute?)
2. Is the external niche legible enough for our action to generate useful learning? (Will we be able to tell whether it worked, and why?)

This is called **state–niche fit**. If the answer to either question is weak, the system doesn't push the business to act anyway. It proposes clarification moves instead.

**It routes every proposed action through a human gate.**

Every action the system proposes is classified into one of four categories:

| Gate | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Auto** | Low-risk, reversible, well-understood. Can proceed without human review. |
| **Draft** | Useful action that should be reviewed by a human before going external. |
| **Escalate** | Judgement-sensitive: involves claims, money, partnerships, strategy, compliance or reputation. Human decision required. |
| **Blocked** | Not permitted under current policy. |

The system never bypasses human judgement for anything that matters. It is designed to make human judgement easier, faster and better-informed — not to replace it.

**It banks only what has genuinely been proven.**

When an action works, Kastel doesn't immediately declare it a permanent truth. It tracks whether the pattern holds across different contexts, different conditions, different timings. Only after a pattern survives this testing is it extracted into the Script Library — the business's growing store of reliable operating knowledge.

This is the Gf → Gc spiral in practice. It prevents the organisation from treating lucky outcomes as strategic insights.

**It is designed to protect sovereignty.**

Kastel is polycentric by design: no single platform, vendor or service is irreplaceable in the stack. The framework maintains clear source-of-truth assignments (each important business fact lives in one authoritative system), keeps data lineage traceable, and is built to be self-hosted or run on infrastructure the operator controls.

## How Kastel Differs from Palantir and Similar Systems

You may have encountered Palantir, which is perhaps the most sophisticated example of enterprise operational AI currently deployed at scale. The comparison is useful because it clarifies what Kastel is actually doing differently.

Palantir's approach can be described as: **make the organisation computable**. Build an ontology (a structured, machine-readable model of how the organisation's data, objects and actions relate to each other), connect it to real operational data, and use AI and analytics to drive better decisions and automation at scale. This is genuinely impressive, and it serves the needs of large, data-rich organisations that can afford to implement it.

The difference is not primarily technical. It is philosophical — specifically, about what intelligence is *for*.

Palantir-style intelligence asks: *how do we model and optimise what the organisation does?*

Kastel intelligence asks: *how do we help the humans at the centre of this business become more adaptive, more truthful, more capable of learning, and harder to capture?*

The table below captures this difference:

| Dimension | Mainstream / Palantir-style AI | Kastel Stack |
|---|---|---|
| **Core aim** | Better automation, decision infrastructure, operational execution | Viable adaptive regulation, preserved human judgement, non-extractive scaling |
| **What intelligence means** | Data + models + ontology + automation | Navigating uncertainty while staying grounded, honest and coupled to the real niche |
| **The environment** | Operational reality to be modelled and optimised | A reciprocal niche the operator must remain coupled to without being captured by it |
| **How learning works** | Feedback improves workflows and models | Feedback tests whether a pattern is genuinely portable and worth banking |
| **Role of automation** | A route to scale and execution | A bounded tool inside human-supervised adaptation |
| **Key risks** | Platform dependency, centralised control, over-automation | False learning, niche decoupling, overload, erosion of judgement |
| **Success measure** | More reliable, scalable operational action | Wider viable action band with truth, trust, judgement and niche fit preserved |

Neither approach is universally right. Palantir's model makes sense for organisations whose primary challenge is operational coordination at scale. Kastel's model is designed for a different kind of entity: founder-led businesses, specialist teams, research-led ventures and independent operators whose primary challenge is *remaining adaptive, honest and sovereign* while growing.

## The Six Design Principles

These six principles define how Kastel thinks about intelligence and why each design decision is made the way it is.

**1. Intelligence as viability, not maximisation**

The goal is not to make the business grow as fast as possible. The goal is to widen the range of conditions the business can handle while remaining truthful, solvent and human-governed. Growth that destroys adaptability is not success.

**2. Intelligence as state–niche fit, not internal optimisation**

A good action is not just internally logical. It must connect to real signals from the actual world the business inhabits. An agentic system that takes a goal, plans steps and executes without checking whether those steps make sense in the specific niche is not intelligent. It is just fast.

**3. Intelligence as entropy–mutual information balance, not pure control**

The system must support enough experimentation to discover new things, and enough discipline to filter out noise and lock in what's real. Neither pure exploration nor pure exploitation is healthy.

**4. Intelligence as Gf → Gc spiral advance, not task throughput**

Completing a task is not intelligence. Converting an uncertain situation into a tested, portable, reliable operating pattern — through careful extraction and review — is intelligence. Kastel measures success by the quality of what gets banked, not the quantity of what gets done.

**5. Intelligence as epistemic skill, not just execution**

Kastel is designed to improve the operator's thinking, not just their output. Every loop through the system makes the structure of reasoning more visible: What is the signal? What could explain it? What would prove me wrong? What is the smallest safe test? This is not incidental. It is a design goal.

**6. Intelligence as anti-capture sovereignty, not platform centralisation**

The system's political-economic stance is explicit: use platforms, do not become dependent on them. Keep truth portable. Keep judgement human. Keep agents bounded. Grow replacement paths before you need them. The intelligence model is not only cognitive — it is structural.

## Who We Are Looking For

Kastel Stack is in early development. The repositories exist, the specification is taking shape, and the first working loop is being prototyped. What we need now is people who want to help build it.

We are not looking for people who want to work on AI for its own sake. We are looking for people who share the underlying concern: that the mainstream trajectory of enterprise AI is producing dependencies, not capabilities; opacity, not legibility; and centralised power, not sovereign adaptation.

Useful contributions may come from many different backgrounds. You don't need to be a senior engineer. You don't need to have built AI systems before.

**What you might work on:**

- Connector specifications: defining how Kastel talks to GitHub, Supabase, Stripe, Brevo, Cloudflare and other tools in a typed, source-of-truth grounded way
- Schema design: what does a well-defined business state map look like? What is an Action Intent, precisely? What fields does a banked script need to carry?
- The G Kernel: the central reasoning and routing engine that receives signals, runs diagnostics and generates Action or Clarify Intents
- Supervision interfaces: how does a human operator review, approve or override proposed actions without being overwhelmed?
- Policy packs: what rules should govern when actions are Auto, Draft, Escalate or Blocked for different business types?
- Ontology packs: how should a training company's state–niche model differ from a craft manufacturer's?
- Script banking logic: what does it take to prove that a pattern is genuinely portable? What is the review protocol?
- Documentation and conceptual writing: making these ideas accessible to people encountering them for the first time
- Critical review: challenging the architecture, the assumptions, the theory — pointing out where the design doesn't yet live up to its principles

**What we value in contributors:**

- Intellectual honesty. We are testing a hypothesis, not defending a product.
- Care about the problem. The people this is for — independent operators, specialist practitioners, founder-led ventures — are building real things. What we build affects them.
- Willingness to work at the level of first principles. The specification is not finished. Some of the hardest design questions are still open.
- A preference for doing less, better. Kastel's own principles apply to its development: bank only what works, clarify before acting, don't ship noise.

## A Final Word

Kastel Stack is a bet on a different trajectory for AI in business. The mainstream trajectory is not going to stop. The platforms are being built, the automation is being deployed, and the dependencies are being created, whether we participate or not.

What Kastel is trying to establish is that there is a coherent, principled alternative — an approach to business intelligence that makes operators more capable rather than more dependent, that banks genuine wisdom rather than automating noise, and that treats the preservation of human judgement as a design requirement rather than an obstacle to efficiency.

That is a harder thing to build. It requires a theory of intelligence, not just a library of AI tools. It requires patience with the difference between a lucky outcome and a portable truth. It requires a political-economic stance that most platforms will find inconvenient.

But it is the right problem to work on. And the people it would most benefit — the founders, specialists, researchers and independent practitioners who are trying to build something real without surrendering their agency — deserve better tools than what currently exists.

We are building those tools.

We would like your help.
